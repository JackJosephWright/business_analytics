Rachel Greenlee Nov 28th at 11:39 AM
Hi team, sharing HW5 for comments/questions/concerns. I haven't done formatting yet so the PDF might be a bit wonky. Things to know about this HW:
  1. We are required to model at least 2 of: poisson, negative binomial, multiple linear regression. However we cannot choose a linear model as our final model.
  2. The original predictive variables are an expert star rating, label appeal, and a bunch of chemical variables.  The variables are not normally distributed, and the chemical variables (other than AcidIndex) had no correlation to the target so were not used in models (further they had unrealistic negative values and had strange distributions so they felt like a wild goose chase).
  3. We are predicting # wine cases sold.
  4. A quarter of the expert star ratings were missing, so we created a new factor variable with the categories of 'no rating', 'one star', 'two star', etc.
  5. The poisson and negative binomials performed so similarly with regard to AIC and prediction accuracy on the training dataset she provided, I just chose the Poisson model that was simplest -> higher expert star rating and label appeal increase sales, acidity slightly decreases sales. I'm not wedded to that choice though.
If anyone wants to add/change things in the .rmd please let me know ASAP - I don't have much more patience for this course myself due to her lack of instruction/feedback so I personally feel more than comfortable with the work here but certainly welcome anyone with higher aspirations to add to this! I do want to get this submitted so I/we can offer help on the final project which is worth much more of our grade.If I don't have any comments by Thursday night I will clean it up as-is, add the predictions for the evaluation dataset, and share a final version for on Friday to submit. Thanks for your time reviewing. (edited)

Quick note - the negative values for chemical variables may reflect sample concentrations below the instrumental detection limit.This is very common in chemical analyses. Given that interpretation, you would be justified in treating these measurements as 0 rather than as errors.I think this is a reasonable assumption given the large number of negative measurements. And that the medians for these variables are often close to 0 - at a glance.Taken this approach, a zero inflation model may be an option? (edited) 
sean connin  2 days ago
Another note -- correct me if I'm wrong (seriously) but our primary concerns in count regression aren't related to assumptions of normality. As far as the covariates go, the key assumption is independence. My sense is that the benefit of a glm is avoiding restrictive assumptions (i.e., variable transformations).
